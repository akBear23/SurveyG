INTRIGUING ABSTRACT:
==================================================
The vast majority of emotion recognition research overlooks the linguistic diversity of our world, leaving thousands of under-resourced languages without crucial annotated data. This paper introduces **BRIGHTER**, the largest and most comprehensive multi-lingual, multi-label, and intensity-annotated dataset designed to bridge this critical gap. Encompassing nearly 100,000 instances across 28 diverse languages, primarily low-resource, BRIGHTER features meticulous human annotation by fluent native speakers, capturing six perceived emotion classes, a neutral category, and a four-point intensity scale. We detail a robust annotation methodology, including Split-Half Class Match Percentage (SHCMP) for reliability and a novel label aggregation strategy. Our extensive experiments, utilizing state-of-the-art Large Language Models (LLMs) in few-shot and cross-lingual settings, provide baseline performance for multi-label emotion classification and intensity prediction. Results reveal that LLMs still face significant challenges in accurately recognizing perceived emotions, especially in low-resource contexts, often performing better with English prompting. BRIGHTER sets a new benchmark for multilingual emotion recognition resources, paving the way for more inclusive Natural Language Processing (NLP) applications and deeper insights into language-specific emotional nuances.

Here's a focused summary of the technical paper for a literature review:

*   **Research Problem & Motivation**
    *   The paper addresses the significant disparity in emotion recognition research, which has predominantly focused on high-resource languages, leaving under-resourced languages with a severe lack of high-quality, annotated datasets \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   This problem is important because emotion recognition is central to numerous NLP applications (e.g., healthcare, dialogue systems, computational social science) and challenging due to the complex, subtle, and subjective nature of human emotion expression across diverse languages and cultures \cite{muhammad2025}.

*   **Related Work & Positioning**
    *   Existing approaches are limited by their focus on high-resource languages like English, Spanish, German, and Arabic, leading to a major research gap for the over 4,000 languages spoken globally, particularly in Africa and Asia \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   This work positions itself to bridge this gap by providing a comprehensive, multi-lingual dataset specifically targeting low-resource languages, unlike previous efforts that lacked such broad linguistic coverage and high-quality human annotation for these languages \cite{muhammad2025}.

*   **Technical Approach & Innovation**
    *   The core technical method is the creation and meticulous annotation of **BRIGHTER**, a collection of multi-labeled, emotion-annotated datasets for 28 diverse languages, primarily low-resource ones \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   This approach is novel due to its extensive linguistic coverage (nearly 100,000 instances across 7 language families), the use of fluent native speakers for annotation, and its multi-labeling scheme that captures six perceived emotion classes (anger, sadness, fear, disgust, joy, surprise) along with a neutral category and a four-point intensity scale (0-3) \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   The data collection strategy combines multiple sources (social media, personal narratives, literature, news, human-written, and machine-generated text) tailored to language-specific availability, and includes rigorous pre-processing and quality control steps \cite{muhammad2025}.

*   **Key Technical Contributions**
    *   **Novel Dataset:** Introduction of BRIGHTER, the largest multi-lingual, multi-label, and intensity-annotated emotion dataset covering 28 languages, with a strong focus on under-resourced languages \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   **Annotation Methodology:** A robust annotation process involving fluent native speakers, multi-labeling for simultaneous emotions, and a four-level intensity scale.
    *   **Reliability Measurement:** Application of Split-Half Class Match Percentage (SHCMP) \cite{mohammad2024} to ensure and report high annotation reliability across diverse languages \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   **Label Aggregation Strategy:** A defined method for aggregating emotion and intensity labels from multiple annotators, considering both agreement and average intensity scores \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   **Empirical Insights:** Provides baseline experimental results for monolingual and cross-lingual multi-label emotion identification and emotion intensity recognition, highlighting performance variability and challenges for LLMs \cite{muhammad2025}.

*   **Experimental Validation**
    *   Experiments were conducted for:
        *   **Multi-label emotion classification:** Both in few-shot settings using five Large Language Models (LLMs) (Qwen2.5-72B, Dolly-v2-12B, LlaMA-3.3-70B, Mixtral-8x7B, DeepSeek-R1-70B) with Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting, and in cross-lingual settings using Multilingual Language Models (MLMs) \cite{muhammad2025}.
        *   **Emotion intensity prediction:** Using systems trained on the intensity-labeled subsets of 10 languages \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   **Key performance metrics:** Macro F1 scores for classification and Pearson correlation scores for intensity prediction \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   **Comparison results:** The experiments revealed that LLMs still struggle with recognizing perceived emotions in text, and for low-resource languages, LLMs perform significantly better when prompted in English \cite{muhammad2025}. Performance varied across languages and text domains.

*   **Limitations & Scope**
    *   **Technical limitations:** LLMs still exhibit challenges in accurately recognizing perceived emotions, especially in low-resource contexts where English prompting yields better results, indicating a need for further research into truly multilingual emotion understanding \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   **Dataset scope:** While extensive, some languages (e.g., English, Afrikaans) lack representation for certain emotion classes (disgust, surprise) due to insufficient instances \cite{muhammad2025}. Intensity labels are provided for only 10 of the 28 languages due to annotator robustness requirements \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   **Applicability:** The dataset is primarily focused on perceived emotions from text snippets, and its applicability to other emotion-related tasks (e.g., evoked emotions, speaker's actual emotions) might require further adaptation \cite{muhammad2025}.

*   **Technical Significance**
    *   BRIGHTER significantly advances the technical state-of-the-art by providing the first large-scale, human-annotated, multi-label emotion dataset for a wide array of under-resourced languages, directly addressing a critical data scarcity problem \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   Its detailed annotation scheme (multi-labeling, intensity) and rigorous quality control set a new benchmark for multilingual emotion recognition resources \cite{muhammad2025}.
    *   The dataset and experimental insights have the potential to impact future research by enabling the development of more inclusive and robust NLP models for emotion recognition, fostering research into language-specific emotional nuances, and facilitating the creation of digital tools that cater to a broader global population \cite{muhammad2025}.
